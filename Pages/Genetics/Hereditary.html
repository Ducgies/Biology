How do Organisms Inherit Genes?

Organisms inherit genes from their parents. But how exactly does this happen?

When an organism has offspring, it passes down its genetic information through cell division, either via sexual or asexual reproduction. Then, when the offspring is ready, it passes down its genes through cell division, thus continuing the process1.

The three types of ways that organisms may undergo cell division are meiosis, mitosis, and binary fission2.

Difference Between Meiosis, Mitosis, and Binary Fission

Mitosis is the process in which cells divide into two, identical cells. Eukaryotes undergo this process with their somatic cells (cells that are not reproductive cells). The process has five main stages: interphase, prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase/cytokinesis3.

(“Mitosis Stages” by Ali Zifan. Licensed under CC BY-SA 4.0/resized from original)

Meiosis is similar to mitosis, but instead of producing two identical cells, it produces four sex cells (called gametes) that contain only half of the chromosomes. Due to the fact that the cell has to produce four sex cells, the original cell has to go through nine steps instead of only five: interphase, prophase I, metaphase I, anaphase I, telophase I, cytokinesis, prophase II, metaphase II, anaphase II, telophase II and cytokinesis again4.

(“Meiosis Stages” by Ali Zifan. Licensed under CC BY-SA 4.0/resized from original)

Binary fission is a way for prokaryotes to reproduce asexually. This process ends up creating two identical cells5.

(“Binary Fission 2” by Ecoddington14. Licensed under CC BY-SA 3.0/resized from original)

Genetic Recombination

Genetic recombination is used To create variation between the offspring and the parents. Cells need to “shuffle” the genes that they will pass on within each gamete. One way that cells achieve this during meiosis is through a process called crossing over.

Crossing over is when two chromosomes that are partnered up (or homologous, one derived paternally and the other maternally) exchange parts with each other. This is achieved by fusing the two different parts through a process called synapsis, which occurs during prophase I6.

(“Synapsis and Crossing Over with Labels” by Christinelmiller. Licensed under CC BY-SA 4.0/resized from original)
