How do Cells Communicate?

Cells can signal to each other and can even receive messages via their environment. 1 They do this by using molecules that are usually called “ligands.” Ligands then have corresponding receiving molecules called “receptors” which can be either located within the plasma membrane (cell-surface receptors) or inside the cell (intracellular receptors). 2

Once the ligands bind to the receptors, it causes the receptor to change its shape, which then creates a cascade of messages down the cell. 3

One example of this are G-protein-coupled-receptors (GPCRs) which are located within the plasma membrane. GCPRs interact with G proteins, which have three subunits: beta, alpha, and gamma. When a ligand becomes attached to a G protein-coupled receptor, the receptor changes its shape (also called a conformational change), thus causing a GTP molecule to replace the GDP molecule attached to the alpha G protein. This then causes the alpha G protein subunit to disconnect from its beta and gamma subunits. Once this occurs, either the alpha or beta-gamma dimer can interact with other proteins, thus causing a cascade of messages throughout the cell. 4

When it comes to intracellular receptors, in order for a cell to receive a ligand, the ligand needs to be hydrophobic, meaning that they repel water. An example of this are hormone molecules, such as testosterone and estradiol. Once they pass through the cell membrane, they can then attach to their corresponding intracellular receptors, which can then change the DNA’s transcription process (transcription is what creates proteins for the cell). 2

Types of Cell Signaling

There are four main types of cell signaling:

Endocrine: Endocrine signaling is one way that cells can communicate with each other from very far distances. Hormones are an example, as they can be transported through the blood system in order to get transported all around the body.

Autocrine: This type of signaling happens when the cell wants to communicate with itself by secreting ligands and then receiving said ligands via its own receptors. 5

Juxtacrine: Juxtracine signaling occurs when cells communicate with each other through physical contact via gap junctions, which are like tubes within the cell that allow signaling molecules to go through. 3

Paracrine: This type of signaling happens when cells need to communicate with each other closely. 5 One way that cells use this type of signaling is when they need to talk to other cells to change their identities of what type of cell they will have to be. 3
